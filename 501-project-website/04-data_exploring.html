1.Data Exploring

Data exploring is an indispensable process. Converting your data to visualizations allows you to bring the human visual cortex into your analytical process, and I often find that this helps me find more insights faster, since the visual component of it makes the insights jump right out at me.

In this section, I visualize the both the newwork and tweets text with various visualization methods. Bar chart, histogram, heatmap are all used to dive deer into our dataset. Therefore, the following machine learning classification problem can be built on the EDA results.

To have a glimpse of how the numbers of followers distribute across the dataset, I compare the non hate speech and the hate speech of this variable. Surprinsingly enough, I notice that follower numbers of both the two labs distributed evenly. This means that this the variable plays little role in predicting the cyberviolence tag.

According to this histogram, I notice that the distribution of numbers of friends of both labels is nearly even, but when friends count between 2000 and 4000, there is some slight difference between the two label. Besides, across the xaxis, numbers of friends for each account distributed mostly below 2000. Too many friends for each user account can be deemed as outliers.

From the heatmap, we can conclude that there is no certain pattern between each pairs of the numerical data. From the boxplot, it should be noticed that the distribution of numbers of likes is right-skewed, and that makes sense. Few tweets gain much more attention than most of the others.

The largest numbers of likes of the hate speech can also mean the tweets that attract the most attention in the social media. If we pick the maximum numbers of likes in the label 1, we can see that the tweet just makes no sense. However, this also reflects the importance of followers. People with more followers become opinion leaders, so it is reasonable that their tweets can attract more attention, even what they say in the tweets make no sense.

From this histogram, we can see that only 10 words for each tweets make up the majority. On the flip side, non hate speech texts are generally longer than the hate speech texts, which is very interesting. People usally text violent speech on Twitter in short sentence? It should be some pattern behind the data.

Finally, by plotting this wordcloud of the hate speech tweets only, many words such as woman, girl, sexist, femanism take over this picture. It also reflects that my dataset can be kind of inblance, because there is little racism kind words.
